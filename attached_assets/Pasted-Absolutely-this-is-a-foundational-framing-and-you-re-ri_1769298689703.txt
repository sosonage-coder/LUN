## Purpose of This Distinction

When designing **balance sheet reconciliation templates**, it is essential to distinguish between **monetary** and **non-monetary** accounts.

This distinction determines:

* Whether balances are **revalued each period**
* How **FX impacts** are treated
* What a reconciliation must **explain vs ignore**
* Which template structure is appropriate for an account

Without this separation, reconciliation templates either:

* Overcomplicate non-monetary accounts, or
* Under-explain FX impacts for monetary accounts

---

## Monetary Accounts

### Definition

**Monetary accounts** are balance sheet accounts whose carrying value is **revalued at each reporting date** when denominated in a currency different from the reporting currency.

### Key Characteristics

* Subject to **period-end FX revaluation**
* FX differences impact **P&L or OCI**, depending on policy
* Balance changes may occur **without new transactions**
* Reconciliation must explicitly explain **FX movements**

### Common Examples

* Cash and bank accounts
* Accounts receivable
* Accounts payable
* Loans and other monetary liabilities

### Reconciliation Implication

For monetary accounts, a balance sheet reconciliation **must**:

* Separate **operational reconciling items** from **FX revaluation**
* Clearly show the **FX impact for the period**
* Avoid mixing FX with timing or error-based reconciling items

This is why **cash reconciliations require explicit FX sections**.

---

## Non-Monetary Accounts

### Definition

**Non-monetary accounts** are balance sheet accounts whose carrying value **does not revalue** due to exchange rate changes, even if the underlying transaction occurred in a foreign currency.

### Key Characteristics

* Measured at **historical cost** or other non-remeasured bases
* FX rate is **fixed at transaction date**
* No recurring FX revaluation impact
* Balance changes are driven by **activity, not currency movement**

### Common Examples

* Prepaid expenses
* Fixed assets / right-of-use assets
* Inventory
* Intangible assets

### Reconciliation Implication

For non-monetary accounts, a balance sheet reconciliation **should not**:

* Include FX revaluation sections
* Attempt to explain FX-driven balance changes

Instead, reconciliations focus on:

* Rollforward logic
* Additions, amortization/depreciation, disposals
* Classification and completeness

---

## Why This Matters for Templates

Balance sheet reconciliation templates are **not interchangeable** across account types.

* **Monetary account templates** must:

  * Explicitly surface FX revaluation
  * Explain currency-driven movements

* **Non-monetary account templates** must:

  * Exclude FX logic
  * Focus on transactional rollforwards and aging

Templates should therefore be designed and selected based on:

* Account classification (monetary vs non-monetary)
* Measurement basis
* Expected sources of movement

---

## How This Applies to the Cash Reconciliation Spec

Cash is a **monetary account**.

Therefore, the cash balance sheet reconciliation template:

* Requires currency selection
* Requires FX rate visibility
* Requires a distinct FX revaluation section
* Must isolate FX from operational reconciling items

This logic does **not** automatically carry over to:

* Prepaids
* Fixed assets
* Other non-monetary balances

Those accounts will use **different reconciliation templates**, even though they coexist on the same balance sheet.
